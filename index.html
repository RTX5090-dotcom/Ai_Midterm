Section 01 — About the Project

Project Overview

Project Title: Smarter Seating: Using AI to Redesign Rest Areas in St. Nicholas Park

Goal: To identify where seating is insufficient in St. Nicholas Park and use AI tools to propose an optimal, community-centered bench and rest area redesign that improves comfort, accessibility, and daily use for all park visitors.

Essential Question: How can artificial intelligence help us identify where people need rest areas most in an urban park — and what does a smarter, more equitable seating solution look like?

Location: St. Nicholas Park — 543 W 138th St, New York, NY 10031. A 23-acre public park spanning 13 blocks through Harlem and Hamilton Heights, Manhattan.

Section 02 — The Problem

Problem Definition

St. Nicholas Park spans 23 acres across 13 city blocks — but its benches are almost entirely concentrated along the main entrance walkways near 135th Street. Hillside paths, the interior, the playground area, the dog run, and the upper park near 141st Street have little to no seating at all. The last major bench installation was nearly 30 years ago.

  • 👴
    Elderly Visitors Cannot walk the full length of the park without a rest stop, effectively cutting them off from large portions of the space.
  • 👩‍👧
    Parents & Caregivers Are forced to stand while watching children play at the playground because no seating exists nearby.
  • ♿
    People with Disabilities Are excluded from interior and hillside areas entirely, with no ADA-accessible rest points along accessible routes.
  • 🕐
    Shorter Visits for Everyone Visitors leave earlier than they would like, reducing community presence and overall park engagement.

Section 03 — Site & Context

Site Understanding

A site analysis of St. Nicholas Park reveals clear patterns in how visitors move, rest, and experience the space — and where the absence of seating creates the greatest burden on daily users.

🚶 Movement Patterns

Most visitors enter from the 135th and 141st Street gates. People cluster near the basketball courts and the main lower path but rarely venture into hillside areas — likely because there are no rest points along the route up.

😕 Comfort & Rest

The park slopes steeply from west to east. Climbing the hill requires significant effort, yet there are no benches at the top or along the incline — making a mid-journey rest impossible.

♿ Accessibility

ADA paths exist near the main entrances, but the interior remains difficult for wheelchair users and the elderly. No rest points exist along the accessible routes to break up the long distances.

👥 Community Need

The surrounding Harlem neighborhood has a high senior population and many families with young children — two groups that are most harmed by the lack of rest infrastructure in this park.

Section 05 — Final Proposal

Smart Solar Bench Network

The selected proposal uses AI-driven foot traffic analysis to place 18 solar-powered smart benches at data-identified locations across the full length of St. Nicholas Park — prioritizing the underserved hillside, interior areas, and zones near existing amenities.

📊

AI Foot Traffic Analysis

Computer vision and sensor data pinpoint exactly which areas see heavy visitor use but have zero seating — making placement decisions based on real behavior, not guesswork.

📍

Smart Placement Algorithm

AI scores every possible bench location using shade coverage, accessibility route proximity, slope grade, and distance from other rest points to identify the optimal 18 spots.

🔋

Solar-Powered & Self-Reporting

Each bench runs on solar power and wirelessly reports usage data and maintenance needs to NYC Parks in real time — keeping benches functional and reducing repair delays.

♿

Accessibility First

6 of the 18 benches are ADA-compliant with armrests, extra width, and flat paved placement — specifically designed for elderly visitors and people with mobility challenges.

Justification: This proposal was selected because it combines data-driven precision with long-term sustainability and the lowest cost-to-impact ratio of the three options — making it the most realistic proposal for NYC Parks to actually adopt and fund.

Section 06 — Reflection & Conclusion

Human Reflection on AI Use

This project used AI tools at multiple stages — from research and site analysis to image generation and proposal development. But AI could not do everything. Here is an honest breakdown of what AI contributed and what required human judgment.

🤖 What AI Helped With

  • Generating realistic images of current park conditions and future redesign proposals
  • Analyzing foot traffic patterns to identify underserved areas of the park
  • Scoring potential bench locations across multiple variables simultaneously
  • Researching the park's history, demographics, and prior renovation records
  • Generating three distinct design proposals for comparison
  • Drafting written descriptions and justifications for each option

🧠 What Required Human Judgment

  • Deciding which problem to focus on — AI cannot feel what it's like to visit a park with no place to sit
  • Choosing the final proposal based on community values, not just data scores
  • Recognizing that elderly and disabled residents are most affected — empathy AI lacks
  • Evaluating which AI-generated images were accurate versus misleading
  • Prioritizing accessibility over aesthetics when the two conflicted
  • Writing this reflection — AI cannot genuinely evaluate its own role in a project

Conclusion: AI is a powerful tool for processing data, spotting patterns, and generating options at a scale no individual could manage alone. But the most important decisions in this project — who to design for, what values matter most, and why — were made by a human. The best park redesigns will always combine both.
